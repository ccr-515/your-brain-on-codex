        <p class="small">Project context</p>

        <h2>Codex shifts your bottleneck</h2>

        <p>

          Codex is not just code completion. It is delegation. When it works, your bottleneck moves away from typing and toward

          reviewing diffs, steering architecture, and deciding what matters.

        </p>


        <div class="callout">

          <div><strong>Thesis</strong></div>

          <div class="small">

            If you treat Codex like a junior engineer you supervise, you ship faster with fewer context switches.

            If you treat it like a vending machine, you get fragile code and false confidence.

          </div>

        </div>


        <h2>Three modes</h2>

        <h3>1) Brain only</h3>

        <p>

          You write everything. You keep maximum control, but you pay the full cost in time, attention, and repetition.

        </p>


        <h3>2) Search driven</h3>

        <p>

          You search docs, stitch examples, adapt snippets. Strong for learning, but often fragmented and slow.

        </p>


        <h3>3) Codex driven</h3>

        <p>

          You specify the task, Codex drafts and iterates, you review and redirect. The loop gets tighter and you can run

          multiple tasks in parallel without losing the thread of what you are building.

        </p>


        <div class="figure">

          <img src="./assets/img/figure.svg" alt="Workflow figure" />

        </div>


        <h2>What we actually measure here</h2>

        <p>

          This page is a workflow map, not neuroscience. So the metrics are developer metrics.

        </p>

        <ul>

          <li>Time to first working version</li>

          <li>Context switches per feature</li>

          <li>Diff size and review load</li>

          <li>Test pass rate across iterations</li>

          <li>Parallel task throughput</li>

        </ul>


        <h2>Where Codex is strongest</h2>

        <ul>

          <li>Turning intent into a first draft fast</li>

          <li>Grinding through boilerplate without draining you</li>

          <li>Keeping momentum while you think ahead</li>

          <li>Producing reviewable changes you can accept or reject</li>

        </ul>


        <h2>What can go wrong</h2>

        <ol>

          <li>Your review quality becomes the ceiling</li>

          <li>Vague prompts produce vague code</li>

          <li>Long tasks need checkpoints or they drift</li>

          <li>“Looks right” is not the same as correct</li>

        </ol>

        <h2>FAQ</h2>

        <div class="faq">

          <details>

            <summary>Is Codex basically making you “dumber”</summary>

            <p>

              No. It changes what you spend effort on. You still need judgment. The skill shifts from typing to specifying,

              validating, and reviewing.

            </p>

          </details>


          <details>

            <summary>What is the fastest way to see value</summary>

            <p>

              Pick one annoying task you keep postponing, delegate it, then do a serious diff review. Repeat until your prompts

              get sharp and your review gets faster.

            </p>

          </details>


          <details>

            <summary>What is the main limitation</summary>

            <p>

              If you do lazy reviews, you will accept bugs at scale. Codex amplifies both your discipline and your sloppiness.

            </p>

          </details>


          <details>

            <summary>What is the core mindset</summary>

            <p>

              Treat Codex like a supervised engineer. You direct. It drafts. You verify. That loop is the product.

            </p>
